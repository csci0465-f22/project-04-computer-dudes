What can regional data tell us about Mental Health in the US?

Plotting Mental Health by County

This choropleth graphs the number of average poor mental health days by county across the United States. Looking at the graph, we determined that West Virginia shows the highest concentration of larger values, and North Dakota seems to rank the best. To get even more specific, we can analyze the most extreme cases in each state: Williams County (North Dakota) and McDowell County (West Virginia). We will use these counties as case examples to convey how social community and workplace experience define regional mental health statistics.

To visualize these phenomenon, we're using a broad data set of sociohealth statistics to analyze various quality of life metrics as they relate to mental health.

McDowell County, WV and Williams County, ND at a Glance

An initial comparison of Williams and McDowell County shows their similarity in many relevant statistics. Many of these similarities come in metrics one may initially point to for impacting mental health of an area. Both areas have similar unemployment and obesity rates, as well as insufficient sleep. This leaves us to focus on other metrics as the cause in the discrepancy in mental health.

Seeing that nothing from obesity, unemployment, or sleep stand out, notice the striking difference in both poverty and social community. Although social community may stand out less, this is just due to the scale of the visualization. In terms of ratio from one to another, Williams County dominates McDowell in this statistic. So, if the residents of both counties are employed at similar rates and in similar physical health, we point the finger at the quality of jobs and social community of each county as being the driving force in the disparity in mental health.

Social Life

Due to the extremely rural nature of each county, one may expect the social aspect of each to be similar. This however, is not the case. Residents of Williams County reported to have interacted with an average of 47 people per day. Although this number may be small compared to the likes of a city, it dwarves the 10 observed in McDowell County. Additionally, we cite Dunbar's Number which claims that a person can maintain 150 relationships as evidence that interacting with 47 people daily may very well provides a healthy social life. This claim is backed up by the strong mental health seen across Williams County.

Job Quality

In addition to social life we a see an impactful disparity across counties in job quality. Poverty may have been a tell tale sign of this, but the disparity goes much deeper that that. Beyond a higher median income, people in Williams County have on average a much shorter commute to work. Further, across both respective states as a whole, workers in North Dakota typically have a much higher satisfaction with their jobs

Putting all of this together, it comes as no surprise that the county where people maintain a healthy social life as well as enjoying a short commute to a well paying job they love each day performs so strongly in mental health measures.
